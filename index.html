Chapter 5: Cellular Respiration Study Notes

Cellular respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose and other organic molecules to produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate), which serves as the primary energy currency of the cell. This process occurs in three main stages: glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain.

Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm and does not require oxygen. During this process, one molecule of glucose (a 6-carbon sugar) is split into two molecules of pyruvate (3-carbon molecules). This process produces a net gain of 2 ATP molecules and 2 NADH molecules.

Key points to remember:

  • Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm, not the mitochondria
  • Net gain: 2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2 pyruvate molecules
  • Does not require oxygen (anaerobic process)
  • 10 enzymatic steps are involved in the complete pathway

The Krebs Cycle (also called the citric acid cycle or TCA cycle) takes place in the mitochondrial matrix. Pyruvate is first converted to acetyl-CoA before entering the cycle. For each glucose molecule, the cycle turns twice, producing 2 ATP, 6 NADH, and 2 FADH2.

The transition reaction (pyruvate oxidation) converts each pyruvate into acetyl-CoA, releasing one CO2 and producing one NADH per pyruvate molecule. This is an important link between glycolysis and the Krebs cycle.

The Electron Transport Chain (ETC) is located in the inner mitochondrial membrane. NADH and FADH2 donate their electrons to the chain, which passes them through a series of protein complexes. This creates a proton gradient that drives ATP synthase, producing approximately 34 ATP molecules through oxidative phosphorylation.
